Overview

SIPS is a mobile-first product I designed to make discovering local beverage spots feel more intuitive, curated, and visually engaging. The goal was to create a platform that helps users find drink shops more easily while also giving small businesses more visibility.

▶ Watch Full Walkthrough

The Problem

Discovering local drink spots often feels scattered. Most users rely on social media, word of mouth, or broad search platforms, which can make the experience feel inconsistent and overwhelming. I wanted to explore how a dedicated platform could make that process simpler, faster, and more enjoyable.

My Goal

My goal with SIPS was to design a product that felt focused and easy to use. Instead of overwhelming users with too much information, I wanted the experience to feel curated and intentional.

I focused on three things:

  • making discovery fast
  • keeping the interface visually clear
  • supporting small businesses through a more dedicated platform

Process

1. Defining the Idea

I started by identifying the core concept behind the app. I wanted to create something centered around beverage discovery, but with a more curated and modern feel than traditional search platforms.

At this stage, I focused on questions like:

  • What problem is this solving?
  • Who is this for?
  • What would make this experience feel different?

This helped me define the product early on as a platform for discovering local drink spots in a cleaner and more intentional way.

[ Insert Image Here: Early Notes, Brainstorming, Whiteboard, or Concept Sketches ]

2. Establishing the Core Experience

Once the idea was clear, I narrowed the product down to its most important actions. I wanted the experience to feel simple from the start, so I focused on the main things a user would want to do inside the app.

The core experience became:

  • browse local beverage spots
  • view shop details
  • explore options quickly and easily

This step was important because it helped me avoid overbuilding and kept the product focused on what mattered most.

[ Insert Image Here: User Flow, Site Map, or Feature Breakdown ]

3. Planning the User Experience

After defining the core functionality, I began thinking through the user journey. I wanted the flow to feel natural and low-friction, so I built the experience around a simple sequence:

Open → Browse → Select → Explore

I considered how users would move through the app, what information they needed first, and how the interface could guide them without feeling heavy.

This stage helped shape the structure of the product before I moved into visual design.

[ Insert Image Here: User Journey Map, Wireflow, or App Flow Diagram ]

4. Designing the Interface in Figma

Once the structure was in place, I moved into visual design. I designed the app in Figma, focusing on creating a clean and modern interface that felt easy to scan and comfortable to use.

Some of the design choices I made included:

  • card-based layouts for easier browsing
  • clear typographic hierarchy
  • consistent spacing and alignment
  • a lightweight visual style that kept the content in focus

I wanted the interface to feel polished, but also practical.

5. Turning the Design into a Working Frontend

After the visual design phase, I translated the interface into code using HTML, CSS, and JavaScript. This step pushed the project beyond concept work and into implementation.

Building the frontend helped me understand how design decisions hold up in a real environment. It also made me think more critically about responsiveness, spacing, and structure outside of Figma.

This phase was especially valuable because it strengthened the connection between my design thinking and front-end execution.

6. Deploying the Product

Once the frontend was built, I moved into deployment. I hosted the live frontend and backend using Render so the project could exist as a real, accessible product rather than just a design file.

This stage made the project feel much more tangible. It shifted the work from mockups and local development into something that could actually be tested in a live environment.

7. Connecting the Frontend to the Backend

A major turning point in the project was integrating the frontend with the backend API. This allowed the product to move from a static interface to a more dynamic experience.

At this stage, I focused on:

  • connecting screens to real data
  • displaying content dynamically
  • making the app behave more like a live product

This was one of the most important parts of the process because it introduced more realistic system behavior and made me think beyond the surface of the UI.

8. Testing on Mobile

After deployment and integration, I tested the app on mobile devices to evaluate how the experience felt in practice. This helped me identify layout issues, spacing inconsistencies, and interaction details that were not as obvious in design tools alone.

Testing in a real environment showed me how important it is to refine for actual use, not just visual presentation.

This stage reinforced the idea that strong product design continues beyond the mockup phase.

9. Key Takeaways

SIPS taught me how to take a project through multiple stages of the product process, from idea to execution. More importantly, it helped me understand how design decisions evolve once a product becomes interactive and real.

Through this project, I strengthened my ability to:

  • define a focused product concept
  • design clear, structured interfaces
  • translate design into code
  • think through real implementation constraints
  • validate the experience through testing
